5 signs something might be fake

Learn to spot the tricks — even without our tool.

It makes you panic

Scammers want you to act before you think. "Urgent!", "24 hours", "or else" — these are pressure tactics.

The link looks almost right

"paypa1.com" not "paypal.com". One wrong letter is all it takes. Always hover before you click.

No one else is reporting it

Real big news appears in multiple known outlets. If only one site is publishing it, be sceptical.

It was forwarded many times

WhatsApp labels heavily-forwarded messages. The more it's been passed around, the less you can trust it.

It asks you to keep a secret

"Don't tell anyone" is a classic manipulation tactic. Legitimate services never ask for secrecy.

How can I tell if a photo has been generated by AI?

AI-generated images often have telltale signs if you look closely: hands with too many or too few fingers, ears that don't match, text in the background that is blurry or nonsensical, and lighting that doesn't behave naturally — for example, shadows falling in opposite directions. Backgrounds can also look soft or strangely repetitive.

The fastest check: describe what you see in our tool above and we'll flag the warning signs for you.

How do I know if a WhatsApp message is a scam?

Most WhatsApp scams share the same playbook. Watch for these red flags:

  • Urgency — "act within 24 hours or lose access"
  • A link that looks almost right but isn't (e.g. "paypa1.com")
  • Requests for money, gift cards, or personal information
  • A "family member in trouble" asking you not to tell anyone
  • Messages forwarded many times (WhatsApp labels these)

When in doubt, paste the message into our checker above — it will give you a plain-English verdict in seconds.

Can AI fake someone's voice in a phone call?

Yes — and this is one of the fastest-growing scams targeting older adults. With just a few seconds of someone's real voice (taken from a video or voicemail), AI tools can clone it convincingly. Fraudsters then call family members pretending to be a grandchild, child, or friend in distress.

If you receive an unexpected call from a "family member" asking for money urgently, hang up and call them back on their known number. Establish a family code word that only real family members know.

What is a deepfake and how can I spot one?

A deepfake is a video or image in which a real person's face or voice has been replaced by AI. They are used to spread false statements attributed to politicians, celebrities, or public figures — or to impersonate ordinary people.

Signs of a deepfake video include: unnatural blinking or no blinking at all, edges around the face that shimmer or blur slightly, voice that doesn't quite match the lip movements, and lighting on the face that differs from the background. Always check whether a reputable news source is reporting the same event before sharing.

How do I check if a news story is fake?

The fastest method: search the headline in a search engine. If the story is real, multiple well-known outlets will report it. If only one unknown website carries it, be very sceptical.

Also check the date — old stories are often recirculated with a new date to make them seem current. Look for an author name and see if that person exists. And watch for emotional language designed to outrage rather than inform. You can also paste the headline into our tool above for an instant assessment.

What should I do if I already clicked a suspicious link or sent money?

Act quickly but calmly:

  • Clicked a link: do not enter any personal information on the page that opened. Close the browser, run your device's security scan, and change passwords for important accounts.
  • Gave personal information: contact your bank immediately and explain what happened. They can flag your account for suspicious activity.
  • Sent money: contact your bank or transfer service right away — the sooner you act, the better the chance of recovery. Report the scam to your national consumer protection authority.
